BLUF: DeepL has no true offline mode — every request travels to its cloud servers, so your text leaves your Mac. If you want a DeepL alternative for Mac that works offline, the real options are Apple's built-in Translate app with downloaded languages, self-hosted LibreTranslate, or a local large language model running through Ollama. For understanding foreign text and writing or replying without anything leaving your machine, [Nugumi](/) paired with local Ollama keeps the entire flow on-device and private.

## Why DeepL can't run offline

DeepL is a cloud service. Even the macOS desktop app sends the text you submit to DeepL's servers for processing, which means it needs an internet connection and your words leave your computer every time. There is no hidden setting that flips DeepL into a fully local, no-network mode. That single fact is why so many privacy-conscious Mac users start hunting for an on-device alternative in the first place.

## What "offline" and "private" actually mean

Offline means the work happens on your Mac with zero network calls; private means the text you select or type is never uploaded to a third party. On a well-chosen local tool these go hand in hand — if the computation runs on-device, your data has nowhere else to go. When you compare any "DeepL alternative for Mac offline" candidate, the test is simple: ask where the processing happens. If the answer is "our servers," it is neither offline nor private, no matter how the marketing reads.

## Apple's built-in Translate app

macOS already includes a Translate app that can download language packs for on-device use. Once a language is installed, it handles text and even live conversation with no connection at all. It costs nothing, it is already on your Mac, and it is the fastest starting point for plain sentence translation when you are offline. The trade-off is that it is a basic translator — it will not explain nuance, rewrite your own writing, or draft a reply.

## LibreTranslate and local models via Ollama

If you want more control, LibreTranslate is an open-source engine you can self-host on macOS and call entirely locally — fully offline once it is running. Alternatively, a local large language model served through Ollama can both translate and explain text on-device, with no account and no cloud. Both take more setup than Apple Translate, but in return you own the whole pipeline and nothing you process is ever sent out. This is the route most people mean when they ask for a private, local translation app on Mac.

## Where Nugumi fits: understand, rewrite, reply

Nugumi is a macOS menu-bar assistant, not a dedicated translator — but it handles the work that sits around the language gap. Select foreign or confusing text and it explains what the message means in plain words; paste your own rough draft and it polishes the wording so it reads clear, natural, and professional; or let it draft a reply in your own style. Point it at a local Ollama model and none of that text ever leaves your Mac. It works inside any app with selectable text — Slack, Gmail, Notion, Safari, PDFs, KakaoTalk — so you never copy into a separate window. ## Building a private, offline workflow

A practical setup pairs two layers. Keep Apple Translate or LibreTranslate for raw sentence translation, and add a local-Ollama assistant for the harder part: making sense of a message and writing a good response. Install Ollama, choose a local model, and connect Nugumi — now understand, rewrite, and reply all run on-device with no signup and no data sent out. ## Choosing the right tool for you

If you only need literal translation while offline, Apple Translate is the quickest answer and it is already installed. If you want full ownership and are comfortable self-hosting, LibreTranslate or a raw local model fits best. And if your real job is understanding incoming messages and replying well — in clear, natural English — without the cloud, a local-Ollama assistant is the better match.
