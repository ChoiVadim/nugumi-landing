BLUF: To ask for a raise in an email professionally, time the request around a clear win, open with your ask in the first line, back it with two or three concrete results, state a specific number or range anchored to market data, and close by inviting a conversation. Keep the message short, factual, and free of ultimatums — confidence comes from evidence, not pressure.

## When and why to ask for a raise in writing

A salary increase email to your manager works best when you tie it to demonstrated value: a finished project, a strong performance review, expanded responsibilities, or a market shift you can point to. Email gives you a written record and the time to phrase your case carefully, which is exactly why many people prefer to request a pay raise in writing first and then talk it through live. Avoid sending it during a hiring freeze, a layoff cycle, or the week of a major deadline. Timing signals judgment, and judgment is part of what you are arguing deserves more pay.

## Build your case before you write

Before you draft anything, gather evidence. List the results you have delivered, ideally with numbers — revenue influenced, hours saved, projects shipped, scope you took on beyond your original role. Then check market data for your title and location so your number is anchored to reality, not a guess. The strongest email asking for a raise reads like a calm summary of facts the reader can verify, not a plea. If you cannot name two or three concrete wins yet, it may be worth waiting a quarter and building them first.

## How to structure a raise request email

Lead with the ask so your manager knows the purpose in the first line — burying it three paragraphs down reads as nervous. Follow with a short bullet or two of your strongest results, then state your specific figure or range and the reasoning behind it. Close by asking for a conversation rather than demanding a yes, which keeps the door open and respects that compensation often involves other people. Keep the whole thing under 200 words; a tight message is easier to say yes to than a wall of justification.

## Words and tone that work, and what to avoid

Confident and factual beats either apologetic or aggressive. Skip hedging openers like "I'm so sorry to bother you" and "I know this might be a lot to ask" — they undercut your case before it starts. Equally, avoid ultimatums such as "match this or I'll leave" unless you genuinely mean it and have an offer in hand, because they change the relationship permanently. Phrases like "based on my results this year" and "I'd love to discuss" strike the right note: assured, specific, and collaborative.

## A raise request email template you can adapt

Use this ask for a raise email template as a starting point and fill in your own details: "Hi [Manager], I'd like to discuss adjusting my salary to reflect my contributions over the past year. Since [date], I've [result one] and [result two], and taken on [new responsibility]. Based on those results and current market rates for this role, I'd like to request a raise to [figure or range]. Could we set up time this week to talk it through?" This email asking for a raise sample is deliberately short — it opens the conversation rather than trying to win it in one message.

## Polish your tone before you send

The hardest part is rarely the facts; it is sounding self-assured without sounding entitled, especially if English is not your first language. ## What to do after you send it

Give your manager a few business days to respond before nudging — compensation decisions usually involve a chain of approvals. If you hear nothing, a short, calm reminder is appropriate; our guide on how to [send a follow-up email without sounding pushy](/guides/follow-up-email-without-sounding-pushy) covers the timing and wording. If the answer is "not right now," ask what specific results would justify a raise next cycle and get that in writing. And if a no comes with a tone you did not expect, resist firing back the same day — the same calm, evidence-first approach you used to ask is what protects the relationship afterward.
