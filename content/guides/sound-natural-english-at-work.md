BLUF: To sound more natural in English at work, stop chasing big vocabulary and start copying how natives actually phrase things — short sentences, common word pairings, and a tone that matches the channel and the reader. Most writing that reads as non-native is grammatically correct; it just carries word-for-word phrasing from another language, even sentence lengths, and stiff openings. Fix the rhythm, lead with your point, match tone to the moment, and read it aloud before you send. Those four habits do more than any grammar drill.

## Natural is clear, not fancy

The biggest myth about sounding natural in English at work is that you need rare, impressive words. The opposite is true. Native professionals write with a small core of common words and keep sentences plain. Reaching for vocabulary you would never say out loud usually makes you sound more foreign, not less, and it slows the reader down. Aim for the version a busy colleague can read in one pass — clarity, not difficulty, is what makes a line sound like a native wrote it.

## Watch collocations, not just grammar

Most sentences that feel off are grammatically correct but use the wrong word pairing. Natives say "do research," not "make research"; "discuss the plan," not "discuss about the plan"; "heavy workload," not "strong workload." These fixed combinations are called collocations, and they are the single biggest tell of non-native writing. When a phrase feels uncertain, search it in quotes and see whether native sources actually use it. Over a few weeks of checking, the corrections start to feel automatic.

## Lead with your point and vary your rhythm

Translated writing tends to bury the point under a long wind-up and produce sentences of similar length, which reads as flat. Put the request, decision, or question in the first line, then add context only if the reader needs it. Native writing also mixes lengths — a short sentence, then a longer one, then short again — so break anything over about twenty-five words into two. This is why [clear Slack messages](/guides/professional-slack-messages-non-native-speaker) land better: the shape does half the work.

## Cut phrasing carried over from your language

Many awkward lines come from translating an idiom or structure directly. "I will revert to you," "kindly do the needful," and "as per our discussion" are correct words in unnatural combinations. Replace them with what natives say: "I'll get back to you," "could you handle this," "as we discussed." The same care applies to tone — over-formal openings can read as cold, and very short replies like "no" can read as curt without a softener like "when you get a chance."

## Match tone to the channel and the reader

Sounding natural is partly about register: the same words read warm or cold depending on context. A quick message to a teammate should sound lighter than a note to an executive, and a public channel calls for a more considered tone than a private DM. When you are unsure, mirror how others in that channel already write. This matters most in the moments where tone is hardest — for example, when you need to [politely decline a request on Slack](/guides/politely-decline-request-slack) without sounding dismissive.

## Read it aloud before you send

Your ear is faster than your eye. Reading a draft aloud catches missing articles, stiff transitions, and lines that run too long, because anything you stumble over is usually the exact spot a native reader would notice. This single habit does more to make your English sound natural at work than any grammar rule, and it costs thirty seconds. Do it for anything that matters — an email to a client, a message to your manager, a doc the team will read.
