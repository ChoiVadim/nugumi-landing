BLUF: To make your English writing sound natural and not robotic, write the way you would calmly explain something out loud—use contractions, shorter sentences, and plain verbs, then cut the stiff connectors ("furthermore," "kindly be informed") that textbooks and machines overuse. Robotic writing almost always comes from three habits: over-formal vocabulary, sentences that are all the same length, and translating word-for-word from your first language. Fix the word choice and the rhythm, read it aloud once, and the text will sound like a person wrote it.

## What makes English writing sound robotic

Robotic text has clear tells. Every sentence is the same length, the vocabulary is needlessly formal, and transitions are bolted on ("Moreover," "Henceforth," "As per my last email"). Non-native writers often inherit these habits from school textbooks or from translating literally, so the result is grammatically correct but lifeless. The reader notices the stiffness even when they cannot name it, and they quietly assume you are colder or more distant than you mean to be. Naming the patterns is the first step to removing them.

## Cut stiff connectors and filler

Words like "furthermore," "thus," "kindly," and "do the needful" instantly signal machine or textbook English. Replace them with "also," "so," "please," and a direct request. Delete throat-clearing openers such as "I am writing to inform you that"—just say the thing. Fewer filler words almost always reads as more human, and it shortens the message at the same time.

## Use contractions and plain verbs

"I will not be able to" becomes "I can't." "We are in receipt of" becomes "We got it." Contractions and everyday verbs are how fluent speakers actually write at work, even in polished [professional work emails](/guides/sound-more-professional-work-emails). Avoid Latinate giants like "utilize," "commence," and "endeavor" when a small word—"use," "start," "try"—does the job. The smaller word is rarely less professional; it is just clearer.

## Vary your sentence rhythm

Robotic writing marches in identical steps. Mix one short sentence with one longer one, and start a sentence with a verb or a short clause now and then. This variation is the single biggest thing that separates a natural [clear sentence](/guides/sound-like-native-english-speaker-writing) from one that reads like a user manual. When the rhythm moves, the writing breathes.

## Match the tone to the situation

Natural does not mean casual, and professional does not mean stiff—they are the same target. A note to a teammate can be warmer than a note to a new client, but neither needs "Dear Sir/Madam, please be advised." Decide who you are writing to, then use the lightest tone that still respects them. Over-formality reads as cold, which is its own kind of robotic.

## Read it aloud before you send

Your ear catches what your eye skips. If you run out of breath or stumble over a clause, the sentence is too long or too stiff. If it sounds like a robot reading an announcement, it is, and you should break it up. Reading aloud is free, takes seconds, and works no matter your first language—do it once before every message that matters.

## A quick before-and-after

Before: "Furthermore, I am writing to kindly inform you that the requested document has been duly attached herewith for your perusal." After: "I've attached the document you asked for—let me know if you need anything else." Same meaning, half the words, and it sounds like a human being. Notice what changed: a smaller verb, a contraction, one clean sentence instead of a wall of formality, and a warm closing instead of "for your perusal." None of it required perfect grammar—just plainer choices. Aim for that gap every time, and your English will stop reading as robotic and start sounding natural.
