BLUF: To reschedule a meeting by email professionally, send the message as early as you know, name the meeting and the reason in one short line, apologize once, and offer two or three concrete new time slots. Keep it under 120 words, stay polite, and make it easy for the other person to say yes to a new time. Knowing how to reschedule a meeting by email professionally is mostly about timing, clarity, and giving the reader an easy next step.

## Send the email the moment you know

Timing matters more than wording. The earlier you tell people, the more options everyone has and the less disruption you cause. If the meeting is within a few hours, send the email immediately and add a quick message on Slack or Teams as a backup. A same-day cancellation with no notice reads as careless; a two-day heads-up reads as responsible.

## Write a clear, specific subject line

Your subject line should let the reader act without opening the email. Use the meeting name plus the word "reschedule" so it is obvious at a glance — for example, "Reschedule: Q3 budget review" or "Need to move our 2pm call." Avoid vague lines like "Quick question" or "Sorry." A specific subject also makes the thread easy to find later when you confirm the new time.

## Follow a simple, professional structure

A strong email to reschedule a meeting has four short parts: the request, a brief reason, new options, and a thank-you. Open with the point ("I need to reschedule our meeting on Thursday"), give one honest sentence of context, propose specific times, and close warmly. You do not owe a detailed explanation — "a scheduling conflict has come up" is enough for most professional settings.

## Apologize once, then move on

One sincere apology is professional; three is anxious. Say "Apologies for the late notice" a single time and let the rest of the email be useful and forward-looking. Over-apologizing makes you look less confident and buries the information the reader actually needs. If you genuinely caused a problem, a separate note to [apologize professionally for a mistake](/guides/apologize-professionally-email-mistake) does that work better than padding the reschedule request.

## Offer two or three concrete time options

The fastest way to lock in a new slot is to remove guesswork. Propose two or three specific windows with the time zone spelled out ("Wednesday 10am or Thursday 2pm KST"), and invite the reader to suggest an alternative if none fit. This turns a back-and-forth into a single reply. If you use a scheduling link, still name a couple of times in the text so the email stands on its own.

## Reschedule meeting email samples

Short notice: "Hi Maria — I'm sorry for the late notice, but I need to reschedule today's 3pm call. Would tomorrow at 10am or 11:30am work? Happy to fit your schedule if neither does. Thanks for understanding."

More notice (reschedule due to a conflict): "Hi team — A conflict has come up, so I'd like to move Thursday's planning meeting. Could we do Friday at 2pm or Monday at 11am instead? Let me know what works and I'll send an updated invite. Thanks."

## Confirm the new time and update the invite

Rescheduling isn't done until the new time is on everyone's calendar. After the other person agrees, reply briefly to confirm and send the updated invite the same day so no one holds the old slot. For recurring meetings, state whether the change is one-time or permanent. A two-line confirmation ("Great — sending an invite for Thursday 2pm now") prevents the most common scheduling mix-ups.

## Common mistakes to avoid

Don't disappear and reschedule silently — always send the email and confirm once the new time is set. Don't pile on excuses; one reason is plenty. Don't forget the time zone, especially with remote teams. And don't leave the next step unclear: end with who is sending the updated invite.
