BLUF: The best Grammarly alternatives for Mac in 2026 fall into three groups: built-in and free options (Apple's macOS Writing Tools and LanguageTool), deep grammar checkers (ProWritingAid), and system-wide AI assistants that work in any app (Nugumi). If you are a non-native professional, the deciding factors are usually cost, privacy, and whether the tool helps everywhere on your Mac—not just in a browser tab. Grammarly is good at catching errors, but many people now want something cheaper, more private, and able to rewrite and explain text, not only flag it.

## Why look for a Grammarly alternative on Mac

Grammarly works, but it has real trade-offs on a Mac. It runs mostly as a browser extension and a separate desktop app, so it does not help cleanly inside every native app. It sends your text to its servers, which is a problem for confidential or regulated work. And its subscription adds up over a year. For non-native professionals, the bigger gap is that grammar correction alone does not fix tone—the thing that most often makes a message land wrong.

## What to look for in a Mac alternative

Judge a Grammarly alternative for Mac on five things: does it work system-wide (in Slack, Mail, Notion, PDFs, not just the browser), what does it cost, where does your text go, can it rewrite and explain rather than only correct, and does it adapt tone to the situation. A grammar engine that lives in one editor is less useful than a system-wide writing assistant for Mac that follows you into every app you actually type in. Weigh these against how you really work: if most of your writing happens in chat and email rather than a single document editor, convenience and reach matter more than the depth of any one grammar report.

## Free and private alternatives

If price is the issue, start with what is already on your Mac: macOS Writing Tools and built-in proofreading are free and work in many apps. [LanguageTool](/guides/best-ai-writing-assistant-non-native-english-speakers) has a strong free tier and a privacy-friendly stance. For a truly private Grammarly alternative, you want a tool that can run on local models so nothing leaves your machine—Nugumi does this with Ollama, which keeps everything you select on your own computer.

## Deep grammar checkers

If you want detailed reports—readability scores, repeated phrasing, style breakdowns—ProWritingAid and LanguageTool go deeper than Grammarly in places. These are best when you write long-form documents and want to study your own patterns over time. The downside is that they are heavier and more browser- or editor-bound, so they are less convenient for quick chat replies and short emails throughout the day.

## System-wide alternatives that work in any app

This is where Grammarly is weakest on a Mac, and where newer tools win. A menu-bar assistant acts on whatever text you select, in whatever app you are in. [Nugumi](/) is built this way: select text and it can explain what something means, rewrite your own draft so it reads clear and natural, or draft a reply in your style—inside Slack, Gmail, Notion, Safari, KakaoTalk, PDFs, and even VS Code. It also adapts tone to the app, and a double-press of Control lets you ask about anything on screen.

## Best for non-native professionals

If English is not your first language, you do not just need fewer typos—you need writing that sounds natural and right in tone. That is a different job from Grammarly's. The most useful alternative is one that can rewrite a stiff draft into something a fluent colleague would actually send, and explain a confusing message before you reply. It should also draft replies in your own voice, so you sound like yourself rather than a generic template.
