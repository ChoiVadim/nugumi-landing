BLUF: To chase an email response politely, wait three to five business days, then send a short, friendly message that adds context, restates one clear ask, and offers a soft deadline. Assume the person is busy, not ignoring you. Keep it to a few lines, stay warm in tone, and make replying as easy as possible. Two follow-ups is usually the limit before you switch channels or gracefully close the thread.

## When to send a polite chaser

Timing is half of the work. For a normal request, give it three to five business days before you chase an unanswered email, because most people are not slow on purpose, they are buried. If a deadline is involved, send your nudge two to three days before it lands, not after, so the other person still has room to act. Chasing too soon reads as anxious, while waiting too long can let the thread go cold.

## Keep the tone warm, not accusatory

The fastest way to sound annoying is to imply the person did something wrong. Avoid lines like "I still haven't heard back" or "as I already mentioned," which quietly assign blame. Instead, assume the best: "I know things get busy, so I wanted to float this back to the top of your inbox." A polite follow-up email for no response works when it gives the reader an easy, face-saving way to reply.

## A simple structure for chasing an email response politely

Use the same shape every time so you do not overthink it. Open with one friendly line, add a single sentence of context so they do not have to scroll, state one clear ask, and close with a soft deadline. For example: "Hi Sam, hope your week is going well. Circling back on the contract draft I sent last Tuesday, would Thursday work for your feedback? Happy to hop on a quick call if that is easier." That is four lines and it does all the work.

## Words and phrases that stay polite

Small wording choices decide whether a nudge feels kind or pushy. Phrases like "when you have a moment," "no rush, but," "just checking in," and "would it help if I" keep the pressure low while still asking clearly. A genuinely polite way to ask for an update by email leaves the reader an easy yes, so end with a question they can answer in one line. If you tend to over-apologize, read [how to follow up without sounding pushy](/guides/follow-up-email-without-sounding-pushy) for the balance between warm and firm.

## How often to follow up before you stop

Two follow-ups after your first email is a safe ceiling for most work threads. Space them out, vary the wording slightly, and never stack three identical chasers in a week. If you are still chasing a client reply with no response after that, switch channels with a quick message or call, or send a polite closing note such as "I will assume this is on hold for now, just let me know when you are ready to pick it up." That protects the relationship and frees your own headspace.

## Adjust the message to who you are emailing

A chaser to a long-term client can be lighter than one to a new contact or a senior stakeholder. Match the warmth and formality to the relationship, and when a thread has gone sideways, lead with goodwill instead of frustration. If your last message actually caused a delay or a mix-up, it can help to [apologize professionally for the mistake](/guides/apologize-professionally-email-mistake) before you ask for anything new, so the follow-up does not feel tone-deaf.
