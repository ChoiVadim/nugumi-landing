BLUF: To introduce yourself in an email to a new team, send a short note in your first day or two with a clear subject line, your name and role, one line on what you will be working on, and a friendly invitation to reach out. Keep it to about 100-150 words, warm and professional, and proofread it before you send.

## Send it within your first day or two

Timing matters more than polish. Send your introduction email within the first day or two, before someone else introduces you or you meet half the team in a meeting. An early note sets the tone and saves you from repeating the same backstory in a dozen hallway chats. If your manager plans to introduce you, ask whether you should still send your own follow-up so people hear directly from you.

## Write a subject line they will open

A vague subject line gets skimmed and forgotten. Be specific: "Hello from Mara, your new product designer" or "Introduction: Mara joining the growth team" tells people exactly what the email is and why it matters. Keep it short enough to read on a phone, since most people triage email on the go. Avoid generic lines like "Hi" or "New here," which look like spam and bury the one detail that earns the open: who you are.

## Open with who you are and your role

Lead with the essentials in the first sentence: your name, your title, and which team or manager you report to. People want context fast, so do not bury it under three lines of pleasantries. One short line of background, such as where you worked before or what you studied, is plenty for a self-introduction email to a new team. Save the longer story for one-on-one chats.

## Say what you will be working on

Tell the team what you will own and how it connects to their work. A sentence like "I'll be handling onboarding flows, so you'll likely hear from me about signup metrics" helps people know when to loop you in. This is the part that turns a polite hello into a useful introduction email to coworkers, because it answers the unspoken question: what does this person do for me? Naming one or two early priorities also signals that you arrive with a plan, which builds quiet confidence on your first day.

## A short template you can adapt

Here is a structure you can reuse: a greeting, one line on who you are, one on what you will do, and one on how to reach you. For example: "Hi everyone, I'm Mara, the new product designer on the growth team this week. I'll be working on the new onboarding flow, so I'll be in touch with many of you about signup screens. I'd love to learn how you each like to collaborate, so feel free to ping me on Slack or grab a coffee anytime." Swap in your own details and you have a finished new employee introduction email.

## Make it easy to connect with you

Close by inviting contact and lowering the barrier to a first conversation. Mention how you prefer to be reached, whether that is Slack, email, or a quick call, and say you would love to grab coffee or a virtual chat. If your company uses a directory or status tool, point to it. A new team member introduction email that ends with an open door gets more replies than one that just trails off.

## Keep it warm, brief, and error-free

Aim for 100-150 words and read it once out loud; if you run out of breath, it is too long. This note is the first writing sample your colleagues see, so small mistakes stick.
