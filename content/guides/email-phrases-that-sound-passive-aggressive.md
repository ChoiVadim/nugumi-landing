BLUF: The email phrases that sound passive aggressive are usually the ones that feel safest to write — "per my last email," "just following up," "as previously stated," "noted," and "friendly reminder." They look polite on the surface, but they quietly point at the reader's mistake or impatience, and that subtext is what stings. The fix is not to add more softeners; it is to drop the jab and say the plain thing warmly. Below are the worst offenders and what to say instead.

## Why a polite-looking phrase still sounds passive aggressive

Passive aggression hides in connotation, not grammar. A line can be spelled perfectly and still carry an unspoken "you should have known this already." Native readers hear that subtext instantly because the phrases are cultural shortcuts, not literal statements. That is why "per my last email" reads as a sigh even though the words themselves are neutral — the meaning lives in what the phrase implies, not in what it says.

## The phrases that quietly carry a jab

A handful of office phrases do most of the damage. "Per my last email" and "as previously stated" both say "I already told you." "Just following up" and "friendly reminder" can read as "why haven't you replied yet." "Noted" or "received" alone sounds like you are ending the conversation coldly. "As I'm sure you're aware" implies the opposite. And "going forward" or "in the future" often lands as a scolding about a past mistake.

## What to say instead

Swap the jab for the plain, warm version. Instead of "per my last email," try "Sharing this again below so it's easy to find." Instead of "just following up," write "Checking in on this — any update when you get a chance?" Replace a bare "Noted" with "Got it, thanks for flagging this." For "going forward," say "For next time, could we...". The information is identical; you have just removed the finger-pointing. These swaps are the same instinct behind learning to [not sound rude in English emails](/guides/not-sound-rude-english-emails).

## Why non-native professionals get caught most often

If English is not your first language, these phrases are doubly risky. You may have learned them from templates or coworkers as "professional" wording, with no signal that they carry an edge. They are also grammatically clean, so a grammar check will never flag them. The tone problem is invisible from the inside — the phrase looks correct and considerate to you, while a native reader hears the subtext you never intended.

## Punctuation and formatting add their own bite

Tone is not only in the words. A one-word reply with a period — "Sure." or "Fine." — can read as clipped or annoyed. Exclamation marks meant to sound friendly sometimes read as sarcasm ("Great!!!"). Bolding or capitalizing a deadline can feel like shouting. When in doubt, write the sentence in full and let warmth come from the structure rather than from punctuation. Plain, complete sentences are also what make your writing [sound like a native speaker](/guides/sound-like-native-english-speaker-writing) instead of terse.

## Check the tone before you hit send

The hardest part is that you cannot feel your own subtext, so the safest move is a second read as the receiver. ## A quick rewrite you can copy

Passive-aggressive version: "Per my last email, the deadline was Friday. Going forward, please review before replying. Noted." Warm, still direct version: "Quick note — just resharing the Friday deadline below so it's easy to find. For next time, it helps if we lock the date in the thread. Thanks for jumping on this!" Same facts, same deadline, zero jab. That cushioning is what separates an email that feels passive aggressive from one that reads as a normal, helpful colleague — and it is the same habit that helps you [sound more professional in work emails](/guides/sound-more-professional-work-emails) everywhere.
