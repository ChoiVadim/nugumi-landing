BLUF: To politely decline a job offer by email, reply promptly, thank the company sincerely for the offer and their time, state your decision clearly, optionally give a brief honest reason, and leave the door open for the future. Keep it short and warm, skip the over-explaining, and the relationship stays intact.

## Reply promptly to the right person

Once you are sure of your decision, do not sit on it. Sending your decline within a day or two lets the employer move forward with other candidates and signals that you respect their time. Reply to the person who sent the offer, usually the hiring manager or recruiter, and keep the original subject line so your message threads cleanly. A fast, courteous "no" leaves a far better impression than a slow, agonized one.

## Lead with genuine thanks

The opening line sets the tone for the whole message. Thank the company specifically for the offer and for the hours their team invested in interviewing you. Naming something real, such as a conversation you enjoyed or what impressed you about the role, makes the gratitude feel sincere rather than scripted. This is the single most important move when you want to turn down a job offer politely, because it tells the reader the relationship still matters to you.

## State your decision clearly

Be direct without being blunt. After your thanks, say plainly that you have decided not to accept the position so there is no ambiguity. Vague phrasing like "I need to think more" only creates confusion when you have already decided. A clean line works well: "After careful consideration, I have decided to decline the offer." Clarity here is a kindness, not a coldness.

## Give a brief, honest reason (optional)

You are never required to justify your choice, but one short sentence often softens the message. Common, low-risk reasons include accepting another offer that better fits your goals, a change in personal circumstances, or a different long-term direction. Keep it high level and never criticize the company, the pay, or the team. One sentence is plenty; a paragraph of explanation invites a counteroffer or an awkward back-and-forth you do not want.

## Keep the door open

Industries are smaller than they look, and the person you decline today may hire you, refer you, or work alongside you in five years. Close with a forward-looking line that you actually mean, such as hoping your paths cross again or wishing the team well on the role. ## Use a copy-paste template

Here is a polite job offer rejection email you can adapt in under a minute:

> Subject: Re: Offer for [Job Title]
>
> Dear [Name],
>
> Thank you so much for offering me the [Job Title] role, and for the time you and the team spent with me throughout the process. I genuinely enjoyed learning about [company].
>
> After careful thought, I have decided to decline the offer, as I am pursuing another opportunity that better fits my goals right now. This was not an easy decision, and I have real respect for what your team is building.
>
> I hope we stay in touch, and I wish you and the team continued success.
>
> Warm regards,
> [Your Name]

Treat this as a starting point, not a script. Swap in real names and details so it reads like you. ## Avoid the common mistakes

A few small errors undo an otherwise gracious message. Do not ghost the employer, do not apologize excessively, and do not list grievances about salary or the interview process. Avoid leaving the decision open in a way that signals you might be talked into it, unless you genuinely are. Proofread for the company name and the job title, since a typo there reads as careless.
