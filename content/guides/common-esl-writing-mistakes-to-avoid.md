BLUF: The common ESL writing mistakes to avoid at work are article and preposition errors, sentences that run too long, phrasing that sounds blunt in English, mixed formal-and-casual tone, and direct translations from your first language that read oddly. None of these come from weak English — they come from a few predictable habits. Once you know the patterns, you can catch most of them in a quick reread, and your writing starts to read clear, natural, and right in tone for the reader.

## Article errors (a, an, the)

Articles are the single most common English mistake non-native speakers make, because many languages do not use them at all. The fix is to check each noun and ask: is it specific (the report we discussed), one of many (a report), or general (reports help us plan)? When in doubt, read the sentence aloud — missing or extra articles usually sound wrong even when the rule is hard to recall. This one habit removes a large share of the small errors that quietly mark writing as non-native.

## Prepositions that don't translate

Prepositions rarely map one-to-one between languages, so translating word by word produces business English writing errors like "discuss about" or "depend of." The reliable fix is to learn verbs and their prepositions as fixed pairs: depend on, responsible for, interested in, reply to. Keep a short personal list of the ones you use at work and reuse them with confidence. Trusting memorized pairs beats guessing from your first language every time.

## Sentences that run too long

Many ESL writers join several ideas with commas and "and," producing one long sentence where two short ones would be clearer. Long sentences are harder to read and they hide grammar slips inside the clauses. As a rule, give each sentence one main idea, and if you need a breath while reading it aloud, split it. Short, declarative sentences also read as more confident, which is part of how you [sound like a native English speaker in writing](/guides/sound-like-native-english-speaker-writing).

## Tone that sounds blunt

Directness that is perfectly normal in many languages can land as cold or even rude in English. "Send me the file" or "You are wrong" are grammatically fine but read sharply. Soften with "Could you send me the file?" and "I think there may be a small error here." These small cushions change the temperature of a message without changing its meaning — and if your emails keep landing harder than you intend, it is worth learning how to [not sound rude in English emails](/guides/not-sound-rude-english-emails).

## Mixing formal and casual registers

A frequent professional English mistake to avoid is switching register inside one message: a formal "Dear Mr. Park" opening followed by "Hey, can you check this real quick?" Pick a level that fits the reader and the company culture, then stay consistent from greeting to sign-off. Watch for casual contractions and slang in otherwise formal notes, and for stiff phrases like "I am writing to inform you that" in friendly ones. Consistency is a quiet signal of fluency.

## Literal translations and false friends

Translating an idiom or phrase directly from your language often produces something that is grammatically correct but reads strangely to a native ear — "make a photo" instead of "take a photo," or "actually" used to mean "currently." These false friends are hard to catch alone because they feel right in your head. Reading your draft as if a colleague wrote it, or having a second pair of eyes check it, surfaces the phrasing that does not quite fit.

## Redundancy and filler

ESL writing often pads sentences to sound polite: "I would like to kindly ask you to please consider possibly reviewing." Each extra word dilutes the point and adds a place for errors to hide. Cut to "Could you review this when you have a moment?" Strong business writing is lean, and trimming filler is one of the fastest ways to [sound more professional in work emails](/guides/sound-more-professional-work-emails) without studying grammar at all.
