BLUF: To give feedback in English without sounding harsh, lead with your intent, name the specific behavior instead of judging the person, soften the delivery with hedging phrases like "one thing I'd suggest" or "it might help to," and close with a clear, forward-looking next step. The aim is to stay direct enough to be useful while warm enough that the other person stays open to it.

## Start with your intent, not the problem

When feedback opens with criticism, the reader braces and stops listening. Begin by naming why you are sharing it: "I want this to land well for you," or "I'm flagging this because the project is close." A short statement of intent signals you are on their side, which makes the harder part easier to hear. This one move softens feedback more than any clever word choice.

## Name the behavior, not the person

"You're careless" attacks identity; "the report had three numbers that didn't match the source" describes something fixable. Specific, observable feedback feels fair because the person can act on it without feeling labeled. Swap adjectives about the person — "disorganized," "sloppy" — for nouns about the work, like "the timeline" or "the formatting." This is the core of how to give constructive feedback in English that people accept rather than resist.

## Use softeners that keep you direct

English gives you a wide set of hedges that lower the temperature without hiding your point: "I'd suggest," "it might be worth," "one thing to consider," "could we try." Pair one softener with a clear ask so the message stays actionable — "It might help to add a summary at the top" is gentle and specific at once. Don't stack too many, though; three hedges in one sentence reads as nervous, not kind. One cushion, then the substance.

## Watch the words that sound harsher than you mean

Non-native writers often pick words that feel neutral in their first language but read as blunt in English. "You must," "obviously," "actually," and "wrong" all carry more edge than intended. Reach for "could you," "I noticed," "I think there may be," and "not quite there yet" instead. ## Structure it so the criticism is the middle, not the end

Order changes how feedback feels. Open with something genuine and true, place the change in the middle, and close with a forward-looking line: "I think a small tweak here makes the whole thing stronger." This is framing, not flattery — it presents the work as in progress rather than failed. A close that points to the next step ("happy to look again once you've adjusted") leaves the door open instead of slamming it.

## Match the tone to the channel and the app

A line that works in a doc comment can sound cold in a one-line Slack message, where there is no room for warmth to come through. In fast chat tools, add a brief softener or an emoji where it fits the culture; in email, you have space for a fuller frame. ## Read it once as the receiver

Before sending, reread your feedback as if it were addressed to you on a stressful day. If any line would sting, rewrite it to be specific and forward-looking — don't just delete it, because feedback that is too soft helps no one. The aim is clarity wrapped in respect, not vagueness. A clear, calm sentence — the kind covered in [writing that sounds natural to native speakers](/guides/sound-like-native-english-speaker-writing) — almost always reads as kinder than a tangled, over-apologetic one.

## Keep a few go-to phrases ready

Having a small bank of phrasing saves you from improvising under pressure. For openers: "Quick thought on this —" or "One thing that stood out to me." For the change itself: "It might be stronger if," "Have you considered," "I'd lean toward." For the close: "Let me know if that makes sense," or "Glad to talk it through."
