BLUF: Professional email etiquette for non-native English speakers comes down to a handful of habits — a clear subject line, a greeting that fits your reader, a direct but warm tone, short skimmable paragraphs, and a sign-off that states the next step. You do not need flawless, native-level English to write emails people respect and reply to quickly. You need to be clear, correct in tone, and easy to act on. This guide walks through the etiquette rules that matter most at work and the small fixes that remove the friction readers quietly notice but rarely mention.

## Open with a clear subject line and the right greeting

Your subject line is the first etiquette signal you send, so make it specific: "Q3 report — review needed by Friday" tells the reader the topic and the action at a glance. Vague lines like "Quick question" get opened late and answered later. Then choose a greeting that fits the relationship — "Hi [Name]," covers most colleagues, while "Dear [Name]," suits formal or first-contact emails. Skip "To whom it may concern" unless you genuinely do not know who will read it.

## Match the level of formality to your reader

Formality is the part of email etiquette that trips up many non-native English speakers, because the conventions differ from their first language. A note to a senior client is more formal than a quick message to a teammate you talk to every day. When you are unsure, start slightly more formal and relax as the other person sets the tone. Mirroring how they write — their greeting, their sign-off, whether they use first names — is the most reliable shortcut to getting the level right.

## Be direct without sounding blunt

English business writing rewards directness, but direct is not the same as blunt. Frame requests as questions — "Could you send the file by Thursday?" reads far better than "Send the file by Thursday" — and add a short reason so the ask does not arrive cold. A simple "thank you" or "I appreciate your help" carries real weight. The same warmth applies in chat, where the etiquette is faster but the tone still matters; the guide on writing [professional Slack messages](/guides/professional-slack-messages-non-native-speaker) covers how it shifts.

## Structure the email so it can be skimmed

Most work email is now read on a phone, so structure is etiquette too. Keep one idea per sentence and one topic per paragraph, and use a bulleted list when you have three or more points. Lead with your main point instead of a long wind-up like "I am writing to inform you that," then put the context underneath. Clean structure also makes your English look more confident, even when the words you choose are simple and familiar.

## Watch the small tone and grammar signals

Two things most often mark an email as non-native: the wrong formality level and small grammar slips in articles, prepositions, and verb tense. Readers rarely point them out, but they register them. A line that feels neutral to you can read as bossy or cold to a native speaker, so reread once asking how it will feel to the person receiving it. Reading the draft aloud catches awkward sentences your eyes tend to skip over.

## Close with a clear next step and a fitting sign-off

Good email etiquette ends by telling the reader exactly what you need and by when: "Please confirm by Wednesday so we can book the room." Then sign off in a way that matches the formality — "Best regards" for formal messages, "Thanks" or "Best" for everyday exchanges. If the email asks for nothing, say so, so no one wonders whether a reply is expected. Knowing how to turn something down is part of this too; the guide on how to [politely decline a request](/guides/politely-decline-request-slack) shows phrasing that stays warm and clear.

## Build a small toolkit and check tone before sending

You do not need a large vocabulary to follow professional email etiquette — you need a few reliable phrases you trust and a habit of checking tone before you hit send.
