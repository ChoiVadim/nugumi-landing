BLUF: A private AI writing assistant that works offline runs the language model directly on your Mac, so the text you select, rewrite, or reply to never leaves the device. For privacy-conscious professionals handling client data, contracts, or internal messages, that means polished, natural writing help with zero words sent to a remote server. The practical way to do this today on macOS is a menu-bar tool paired with a local model through Ollama — and that is exactly how [Nugumi](/) can run when you choose the on-device engine.

## What "works offline" really means

An offline AI writing assistant keeps both the model and your text on your own hardware. After you download the model once, no part of your draft travels over the network — there is no API call, no server log, and no third party in the loop. This is different from a cloud tool that simply caches results; here the actual generation happens locally. If a tool still phones home for the core writing step, it is not truly private, no matter how it is marketed.

## Why privacy-conscious professionals want offline AI

Most writing help is fine in the cloud, but some of it is not. Lawyers, consultants, healthcare staff, and anyone under an NDA routinely draft text that should never sit on an external server. A private writing assistant that keeps data offline removes the data-retention question entirely: there is nothing to retain because nothing was sent. It also keeps working on a plane, in a secure facility, or anywhere the connection is locked down — useful when the document is exactly the kind you cannot risk uploading.

## How a private AI writing assistant runs on your Mac

The common setup pairs a lightweight menu-bar app with a local model runtime called Ollama. Ollama downloads an open model — something in the small-to-mid size range runs well on Apple Silicon — and serves it on your machine. The writing app sends your selected text to that local model and shows the result, all without a connection. If you want the full picture of model choices and hardware fit, the [local LLM for writing on Mac](/guides/local-llm-for-writing-mac) guide walks through it in detail.

## What you can do offline: understand, rewrite, reply

Three jobs cover most daily writing, and all of them work on-device. Understand: select a confusing sentence and get a plain-words explanation, so jargon stops slowing you down. Rewrite: polish your own draft so it reads clear, natural, and professional without losing your meaning. Reply: draft a response to a message in your own style, then edit to taste. With Nugumi pointed at a local model, each of these runs entirely on your Mac, inside whatever app you are already typing in.

## Offline vs. cloud: when each makes sense

Offline is the right default for anything sensitive — client names, legal text, financials, or unreleased work. Cloud (a ChatGPT or Claude subscription, or your own API key) still has an edge on the hardest reasoning and the largest context, and it needs no local hardware. The smart move is to match the engine to the document: local for confidential drafts, cloud for routine messages where speed matters more than secrecy. A good tool lets you switch between the two without changing how you work, a trade-off the [offline AI writing app with no internet](/guides/offline-ai-writing-app-no-internet) guide explores further.

## Works in every app, not one window

The point of a menu-bar assistant is that it lives everywhere you type. An offline tool should fix a sentence in Slack, Gmail, Notion, Safari, KakaoTalk, a PDF, or VS Code without making you copy text into a separate editor. That matters even more offline, because the whole reason you went local was to avoid moving sensitive text around — including out of the app it lives in. Select, fix, done, with nothing leaving the window or the machine.

## How to set it up

Install the menu-bar app, install Ollama, and pull one model; the [download](/download) page covers the steps for Mac. Point the app at the local model and you are private by default — no signup, no account, and no data leaving your computer. Test it on real work first: rewrite an email, explain a tricky paragraph, draft a reply, and confirm the quality fits before you rely on it. If a routine message ever needs the strongest model, switch to a cloud engine for that one task, then switch back. The control stays with you.
