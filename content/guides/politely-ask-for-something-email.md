BLUF: To politely ask for something in an email, state your request plainly in the first two sentences, explain why you need it, and give a clear but reasonable deadline — then close with a short thank-you. Politeness is mostly clarity plus consideration: make the reader's job easy, respect their time, and use one softening phrase instead of burying the ask under apologies. A direct, considerate request gets a yes far more often than a long, hedged one.

## Lead with a clear, specific request

The fastest way to sound rude is to make someone hunt for what you actually want. Put your request near the top, in one plain sentence: "Could you review the attached draft and send feedback by Thursday?" Specifics — what you need, by when, and in what form — read as respectful because they save the reader effort. Vague asks like "let me know your thoughts when you get a chance" feel polite but quietly push the work of deciding onto the other person.

## Give a reason and a realistic deadline

People say yes more readily when they understand why. Add a short reason: "so we can finalize the deck before the client call." Pair it with a deadline that is firm but humane — name the date and, if it is tight, explain what is driving it. "To stay on schedule, could you confirm by Wednesday?" lands far better than "ASAP," which reads as pressure without context and leaves the reader guessing what urgent actually means.

## Soften the ask without losing clarity

Politeness comes from one well-placed softener, not a pile of them. "Would you be able to…", "I'd appreciate it if you could…", or "When you have a moment, could you…" all turn a command into a request — pick one, then state the ask directly. Stacking "sorry to bother you, I know you're busy, it's totally fine if not…" makes you sound unsure and forces the reader to spend energy reassuring you. Confident is not the same as demanding; you can be warm and still be clear.

## Polite request phrases you can reuse

Keep a small set of go-to openers and closers so you are not rewriting tone from scratch each time. For the ask: "Could you…", "Would it be possible to…", "I'd be grateful if you could…". For the close: "Thank you in advance," "Happy to clarify anything," or "Let me know if that works for you." These flex across Slack, Gmail, and Notion — you adjust the formality, not the underlying structure. ## Match your tone to the relationship and the app

A request to your manager reads differently from one to a teammate you message every day. Closer relationships allow shorter, warmer phrasing; senior or external contacts call for a little more structure and a clearer subject line. The channel matters too — a one-line Slack ask can be casual, while an email to a client deserves a proper greeting and sign-off. ## Common mistakes that make requests sound rude

A few patterns quietly sink polite requests. Burying the ask under three paragraphs of background; using "just" and "quick" to minimize work that is not actually quick; and demanding without a deadline, so the reader cannot plan. Over-apologizing is its own trap — it shifts the emotional labor onto the recipient.
