BLUF: To write an email to schedule a meeting, use a subject line that names the topic, open with one sentence on why you're meeting, propose two or three specific time slots with your time zone, and close with a clear next step. That structure removes guesswork for the reader and gets you a faster yes. Below is the exact format, a reusable template, and the mistakes that quietly delay replies.

## Start with a subject line that names the meeting

The subject line decides whether your meeting request email gets opened today or buried. State the purpose and the ask together: "Meeting request: onboarding plan (30 min)" or "Quick sync on the launch timeline — Thu or Fri?" Avoid vague lines like "Touching base" or "Hello." A specific subject tells a busy reader what you need and how long it will take before they even open the message.

## Open with why you're writing

The first sentence should answer "why am I being asked to meet?" Lead with the reason, not a long warm-up. For example: "I'd like to align on the Q3 budget before the board review next week." When you ask for a meeting via email, one clear sentence of context is enough — the reader can decide instantly whether it's relevant and who else should join.

## Propose specific times, not "whenever works"

The single biggest fix for any email to set up a meeting is to offer concrete options. List two or three slots with the date, time, and your time zone: "Would Tuesday 2pm or Wednesday 10am (KST) work?" Open-ended phrasing forces the other person to do the scheduling work, which means more back-and-forth and slower replies. If calendars are tight, add "Happy to work around your schedule if neither fits."

## Keep the body short and skimmable

A professional meeting request email is three or four sentences, not a wall of text. State the purpose, the proposed times, the expected length, and where you'll meet (Zoom, Google Meet, or in person). Bullet points for the time options help the reader scan in seconds. The shorter and clearer the email, the easier it is to reply on the first read.

## Make the next step effortless

Close by telling the reader exactly what to do: "Let me know which time works and I'll send the calendar invite." Include a meeting link or agenda if you have one. Removing every small decision — who books, what platform, how long — is what turns a maybe into a confirmed slot. If you're requesting a meeting with someone senior, add a line offering to adjust to their calendar.

## A template you can reuse

Use this as your schedule-a-meeting email template and adapt the details:

> Subject: Meeting request: [topic] ([length])
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'd like to meet to [reason in one sentence]. Would any of these work for you?
>
> - [Day, date, time + time zone]
> - [Day, date, time + time zone]
>
> The meeting should take about [X minutes], over [Zoom/in person]. Let me know which slot fits and I'll send the invite.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

## Common mistakes that delay a yes

A few habits quietly cost you replies: no time zone (the reader can't tell when 2pm is), no time estimate (they don't know if they can spare 15 minutes or an hour), and burying the request under a long preamble. Tone slips also hurt — sounding too demanding with a senior colleague, or too vague with a peer. If your first email goes unanswered, send a short [follow-up that doesn't sound pushy](/guides/follow-up-email-without-sounding-pushy) after two or three business days.
