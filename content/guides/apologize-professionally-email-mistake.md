BLUF: To apologize professionally in an email for a mistake, do four things in order: name the specific mistake, take responsibility in one plain sentence, state the fix or next step, and close briefly. Keep it short, factual, and free of repeated apologies. The reader cares far more about how you are fixing it than about how sorry you feel, so spend most of the email on the correction.

## When an apology email is the right move

Send a written apology when the mistake affected someone's work, a deadline, a number, or a decision, and when a record of the correction is useful. For a tiny slip in a live chat, a quick "my mistake, fixing now" is enough. Email is the right channel when the issue needs a clear paper trail or reaches people who were not in the original conversation. If the mistake is serious or emotional, a short call first, followed by a confirming email, often lands better than email alone.

## The four-part structure that works

A professional apology email for a mistake follows a reliable shape. First, acknowledge the specific error so the reader knows exactly what you mean. Second, take responsibility in one sentence, without blaming tools, teammates, or timing. Third, give the fix: what you have already corrected, or what you will do and by when. Fourth, close briefly and offer to answer questions. This order keeps the message calm and forward-looking instead of dwelling on the error.

## Words and phrases that sound right

Say sorry once, clearly, and early: "I apologize for the error in yesterday's report." Then move to action verbs: "I have corrected," "I will resend," "to prevent this, I have." Avoid stacking apologies ("so sorry, really sorry, my deepest apologies") because it sounds anxious and makes the reader focus on your feelings rather than the fix. Skip vague phrasing like "if this caused any issues" — name the impact directly if there was one. For non-native writers especially, plain, short sentences read as more confident than long, hedged ones.

## A short example you can adapt

Subject: Correction — updated Q2 figures attached

"Hi Dana, I apologize for the error in the Q2 summary I sent this morning; the revenue total was overstated by one row. I have attached the corrected version, and the new total is accurate. To avoid this again, I have added a final reconciliation check before sending. Please let me know if you have any questions." Notice it names the mistake, owns it once, delivers the fix, and stops. That is the whole job of how to apologize professionally in an email for a mistake.

## Mistakes to avoid when you apologize

Do not over-explain. A long story about why the error happened reads as an excuse and buries the correction. Do not go silent and hope no one notices — a small, prompt apology costs far less trust than a discovered, hidden mistake. Do not promise more than you can deliver just to sound contrite. And do not bury the apology at the bottom of a long thread; put it near the top where the reader sees it immediately. ## After you hit send

A clean apology usually ends the matter, so resist the urge to keep checking in. If the mistake created follow-up work for someone, give them room and only nudge once if you truly need a response — the same restraint that makes a [follow-up email work without sounding pushy](/guides/follow-up-email-without-sounding-pushy) applies here. Most importantly, do the thing you promised. A professional apology email is only as good as the corrected file, the met deadline, or the new check you said you would add. Deliver that, and the mistake becomes a moment that built trust instead of spending it.
