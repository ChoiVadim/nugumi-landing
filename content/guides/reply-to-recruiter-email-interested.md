BLUF: To reply to a recruiter email when you're interested, respond within 24 hours, thank them, clearly confirm your interest, offer two or three time windows for a call, and ask one or two focused questions about the role. Keep it warm, concise, and professional. Below are the exact steps, a response template you can adapt, and tone tips so your reply lands well.

## Respond quickly, even if you need time

Recruiters usually reach out to several candidates at once, so a prompt reply keeps you visible and signals reliability. Aim to respond within 24 hours, even if it's only to confirm interest and propose call times. If you need a day to research the company before committing to a conversation, say so briefly rather than going silent. Knowing how to respond to a recruiter on email starts with speed: a quick, polite acknowledgment makes a stronger first impression than a perfect reply that arrives a week later.

## What to include when a recruiter contacts you

A strong reply to a recruiter email when interested has four parts: a thank-you, a clear statement of interest, your availability, and a focused question. Thank them for reaching out, confirm the role genuinely appeals to you, offer two or three windows for a short call, and ask one specific thing about the position or hiring process. That structure answers the recruiter's real question — are you in and when can we talk? — and removes the back-and-forth that slows things down. Deciding what to say when a recruiter contacts you is mostly about hitting those four points cleanly.

## A recruiter email response template you can adapt

Here's a response template you can adjust to fit the role:

"Hi [Name], thanks for reaching out about the [Role] position at [Company] — it sounds like a strong fit for what I'm looking for, and I'd love to learn more. I'm available [two or three time windows] for a quick call. In the meantime, could you share [the salary range / a bit about the team / the next steps]? Looking forward to connecting."

Swap in real details so it doesn't read as copy-paste. Recruiters review dozens of replies a week and spot a generic one instantly, so one specific line about why the role interests you goes a long way.

## Match the tone to the recruiter and the role

Tone is half the message. A startup recruiter messaging you on LinkedIn often writes casually, while a corporate or executive-search email usually calls for something more formal. Mirror the recruiter's greeting, sign-off, and level of formality while staying professional. For job seekers writing in a second language, responding to recruiter outreach professionally is as much about tone as grammar — the recruiter is already forming an impression of how clearly you communicate, so a reply that reads natural and confident works in your favor.

## Ask the right questions without overloading

It's perfectly normal to ask about salary, location, or whether the role is remote — recruiters expect these and would rather answer early than waste everyone's time. Keep it to one or two questions in your first reply, though, so you don't seem demanding before you've even spoken. Save the deeper questions for the call. If pay is a dealbreaker, ask plainly: "Could you share the salary range so we can confirm it's aligned before we talk?" That's direct without being pushy.

## Common mistakes to avoid

Don't oversell yourself, don't apologize for "bothering" them, and don't paste your whole résumé into the email. Avoid vague replies like "sure, sounds good" that force the recruiter to chase you for times. If you're interested but can't move right now, say so honestly instead of going quiet — you can always [follow up later without sounding pushy](/guides/follow-up-email-without-sounding-pushy). And if the specific call time they proposed doesn't work, it's fine to [politely decline that meeting invite](/guides/politely-decline-meeting-invite-email) while making clear you still want to talk.

A reply with typos, the wrong company name, or an awkward tone can undercut an otherwise strong candidate.
