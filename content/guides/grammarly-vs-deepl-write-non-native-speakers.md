BLUF: For non-native English speakers, Grammarly and DeepL Write solve different halves of the same problem. Grammarly is stronger at catching grammar mistakes and flagging tone across the apps you already use; DeepL Write is stronger at rephrasing a sentence so it reads fluent and natural. If your main worry is correctness and tone alerts, start with Grammarly. If you can write correctly but your phrasing feels stiff or non-native, DeepL Write helps more. Many professionals end up wanting both jobs in one place — which is where a third approach comes in.

## What Grammarly does well for non-native speakers

Grammarly checks grammar, spelling, and punctuation in real time, and it works almost everywhere you type through a browser extension and a desktop app. For non-native writers, its biggest value is catching the small errors — articles, prepositions, verb tense — that quietly mark writing as non-native. It also flags tone, telling you when a message reads as too blunt or too formal before you hit send. The free tier covers the basics, while deeper rewrites and clarity suggestions sit behind a paid plan.

## What DeepL Write does well for non-native speakers

DeepL Write, from the team behind the DeepL translator, focuses on rephrasing rather than only correcting. You paste a sentence and it returns more fluent versions, with alternative word choices you can click through. For non-native speakers, this is the tool that fixes the "technically correct but sounds off" problem — the phrasing a native speaker would never actually use. It is strongest inside its own editor and supports a handful of languages, so it suits people who think in their first language and want the English to read smoothly.

## Grammarly vs DeepL Write: the key differences

The simplest way to frame grammarly vs deepl write for non-native speakers is correctness versus fluency. Grammarly is a proofreader that lives everywhere and tells you what is wrong; DeepL Write is a rephrasing tool that lives in its editor and shows you better ways to say the same thing. Grammarly covers more surface area — any app, real-time, tone alerts — while DeepL Write goes deeper on a single sentence's natural phrasing. Pricing differs too: both offer a free tier with limits, and both gate their best output behind a subscription, so weigh how often you will actually use each. Neither explains text you do not understand, and neither drafts a full reply for you from scratch.

## Where both tools fall short for non-native professionals

Both assume you already know roughly what you want to say. Neither helps when you read a message and are unsure what it means, or when you need to write a reply in your own voice from nothing. Both also pull your text toward a generic, polished register — useful, but it can flatten the way you sound. Privacy is another gap: your drafts are processed in the cloud, which matters when you write about clients, contracts, or internal plans. And both add some friction — you switch to an editor or wait for a panel — instead of meeting you in the exact window where you are typing.
