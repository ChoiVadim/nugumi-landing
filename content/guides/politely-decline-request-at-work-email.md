BLUF: To politely decline a request at work in an email, thank the person, say no clearly, give one short honest reason, and offer an alternative or next step when you can. A good decline is three to five sentences — warm, direct, and firm. The most common mistake is being vague to seem nice, which leaves the door open and creates more back-and-forth. A clean "no, but here is what I can do" respects everyone's time and protects your own.

## Start with appreciation, then be direct

Open by acknowledging the request and the person behind it. A line like "Thanks for thinking of me for this" signals respect before you deliver the answer. Then say no clearly in the same email — don't bury it under three paragraphs of hedging. People read "I'm not sure I can..." as a maybe, which is worse than a clean decline because it invites another round of asking. Leading with warmth also protects the relationship: the person feels valued even though the answer is no, which is exactly what makes a decline feel polite rather than cold.

## Use a simple structure you can reuse

Most ways to politely decline a request at work in an email follow the same shape: thank, decline, reason, alternative, close. For example: "Thanks for the invite to lead this. I won't be able to take it on right now — my plate is full through the quarter. I'd be glad to review the draft if that helps." This structure works for project requests, extra tasks, deadlines you can't meet, and favors. Keep each part to one sentence so the message stays easy to scan.

## Give a short, honest reason, not an excuse

A brief reason makes your no feel fair rather than dismissive. You don't owe a detailed justification; "I'm at capacity," "this is outside my focus right now," or "the timing doesn't work" are enough. Avoid long explanations, which can sound defensive and quietly invite negotiation. When you must turn down a task at work politely, one honest sentence reads as more professional than a paragraph of apologies.

## Offer an alternative when you genuinely can

A no lands better when paired with a small yes. Suggest another person, a later time, a smaller scope, or a useful resource: "I can't join the full project, but I'm happy to give feedback on the plan." Only offer what you actually mean — a fake alternative creates the same back-and-forth as a vague no. If you truly can't help, it is fine to decline cleanly without one.

## Match the tone to the relationship and the app

A decline to your manager reads differently from one to a peer in Slack or a vendor over email. With a manager, acknowledge the priority and ask what to deprioritize if needed, so the no reads as a trade-off. With a peer, you can be warmer and shorter. ## Avoid the phrases that quietly weaken your no

Skip "I'll try," "maybe later," and "we'll see" unless you mean them — they read as yes. Don't over-apologize; one "sorry" is plenty, and repeating it makes you sound guilty for a reasonable boundary. Watch for accidental openings like "let me know if you still need me" when you have already declined. If you genuinely want to revisit later, say exactly when, so the door is open on your terms, not left ajar by mistake. A firm, friendly close such as "Thanks for understanding" ends the exchange instead of restarting it.
