BLUF: To avoid article and preposition mistakes in English writing, learn a few patterns instead of translating from your first language. For articles, ask whether each noun is specific (use "the"), one of many (use "a/an"), or general (often no article). For prepositions, memorize verbs and their prepositions as fixed pairs — "depend on," "responsible for" — rather than guessing. These errors almost never block understanding, but they quietly mark writing as non-native. A short reread, ideally aloud, catches most of them before you hit send.

## Why these two mistakes are so common

Articles and prepositions are the most common English errors for non-native speakers, and for a clear reason: they rarely exist the same way in other languages. Many languages have no articles at all, so "a, an, the" feel optional or invisible. Prepositions almost never map one-to-one, so a word that means "on" in your language might be "at" or "in" in English. Because there is no reliable rule to translate from, the only fix is to learn English's own patterns — which is very doable once you know what to look for.

## When to use a, an, and the

The core question for every noun is: does the reader already know exactly which one I mean? If yes, use "the" (the report we discussed, the manager). If it is one of many and new to the reader, use "a" or "an" (a report, an update). If the noun is general — a plural or an uncountable idea — you often use no article at all (reports help us plan, feedback is welcome). When you are unsure, read the sentence aloud; a missing or extra article usually sounds wrong even when the grammar rule is hard to recall on the spot.

## Prepositions that don't translate

Translating prepositions word by word is what produces classic errors like "discuss about the plan," "depend of the team," or "responsible of the project." The reliable fix is to stop treating the preposition as separate and learn it glued to its verb: depend on, responsible for, reply to, interested in, good at, arrive at. Keep a short personal list of the pairs you actually use at work and reuse them with confidence. Trusting a memorized pair beats guessing from your first language every single time.

## Build a personal list of fixed pairs

You do not need to master every preposition in English — you need the few dozen that show up in your daily writing. Each time a colleague or a trusted source uses a verb-preposition pair you were unsure about, add it to a note: "follow up on," "get back to," "in line with," "by the deadline." Reviewing that short list is far more efficient than studying grammar tables, because it targets the exact phrases your work requires. Over a few weeks the right preposition starts to feel automatic, and the corrections stop being conscious decisions.

## Read it aloud to catch the small errors

Your ear is faster than your eye for these particular mistakes. Reading a draft aloud surfaces missing articles, doubled articles, and prepositions that don't quite fit, because anything that sounds slightly off is usually exactly the line a native reader would notice. This thirty-second habit does more for article and preposition accuracy than any rule sheet. It also catches the broader rhythm problems that make writing feel translated, which is a big part of how you [sound like a native English speaker in writing](/guides/sound-like-native-english-speaker-writing).

## Don't let small errors undercut your tone

Article and preposition slips rarely cause real confusion, but they add up. A message full of tiny grammar fixes can look rushed even when the content is careful, and that impression bleeds into how your tone is read. Cleaning up these errors is one of the quietest, fastest ways to [sound more professional in work emails](/guides/sound-more-professional-work-emails) — no advanced vocabulary required. Accuracy and tone work together: correct, lean sentences simply read as more confident and more considered.
