BLUF: To ask for a raise in an email to your manager, send a short message that opens with the request, backs it with two or three concrete results, states a specific number or range anchored to market data, and ends by asking for a time to talk. Write it for the person who has to advocate for you upward, keep it factual and free of ultimatums, and treat the email as the start of the conversation rather than the whole negotiation.

## Why email your manager first

A salary increase email to your manager works because it gives them something they can act on: a clear request, the evidence behind it, and a written record they can forward up the chain. Your manager rarely approves a raise alone — they take your case to their own boss, HR, or a budget owner. Emailing first means you hand them a ready-made argument instead of putting them on the spot in a one-on-one. For many roles, the strongest approach is a tight email to set the agenda, then a live discussion to settle the number.

## Time the request around a clear win

The best time to email your manager about a raise is a moment when your value is fresh and visible: just after a positive performance review, a shipped project, a new responsibility, or a market shift you can point to. Timing is itself an argument — it shows judgment, which is part of what you are claiming deserves more pay. Avoid sending the message during a hiring freeze, a layoff cycle, or the week of a major deadline, when even a strong case will read as poorly timed.

## Build the case your manager can repeat

Before you draft anything, gather evidence your manager can quote without editing. List results with numbers where you can — revenue influenced, hours saved, projects delivered, scope you took on beyond your original role — and check market data for your title and location so your figure is anchored, not guessed. The goal is an email so clear that your manager can paste your wins straight into their own pitch upward. If you cannot name two or three concrete results yet, it may be worth waiting a quarter and building them first.

## Structure a raise request email to your manager

Lead with the ask in the first line so the purpose is obvious — burying it three paragraphs down reads as nervous. Follow with a short bullet or two of your strongest results, state your specific figure or range and the reasoning behind it, then close by asking for a time to talk rather than demanding a yes. Keep the whole thing under 200 words; a tight, scannable message is far easier for a busy manager to act on than a wall of justification. The same directness that helps in [professional Slack messages](/guides/professional-slack-messages-non-native-speaker) applies here: say the main thing first.

## Get the tone right, especially in a second language

Confident and factual beats both apologetic and aggressive. Skip openers like "I'm so sorry to bother you" — they undercut your case before it starts — and avoid ultimatums such as "match this or I'll leave" unless you mean it and have an offer in hand. ## A raise request email to manager sample

Adapt this and fill in your own details: "Hi [Manager], I'd like to discuss adjusting my salary to reflect my contributions this past year. Since [date], I've [result one] and [result two], and taken on [new responsibility]. Based on those results and current market rates for this role, I'd like to request an increase to [figure or range]. Could we find time this week to talk it through?" This sample is short on purpose — it opens the conversation and gives your manager a clean argument to carry forward, rather than trying to win the raise in one message.

## What to do after you hit send

Give your manager a few business days before nudging — compensation decisions usually move through several approvals. If the answer is "not right now," ask what specific results would justify a raise next cycle and get that in writing, so you have a concrete target instead of a vague maybe. And if the reply lands harder than you expected, resist firing back the same day; the calm, evidence-first tone you used to ask is exactly what protects the relationship afterward.
