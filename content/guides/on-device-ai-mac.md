BLUF: On-device AI for Mac means the model runs directly on your own computer, so the text you select, rewrite, or ask about never leaves your machine. That single fact is why it matters for privacy: there is no server to trust, no transmitted copy to leak, and no privacy policy to take on faith. If you work with contracts, client data, unreleased plans, or anything under an NDA, on-device processing turns "we promise not to peek" into "your text physically never left." This guide explains what on-device AI actually is, how it differs from cloud AI, what it protects, and how to start using it on a Mac.

## What "on-device" actually means

On-device AI runs the model — the part that does the thinking — on your Mac's own processor and memory rather than in a data center. When you ask it to explain a sentence or polish a draft, the work happens locally and the answer comes back without a round trip to the internet. This is different from a privacy policy that says a company won't store your data; with on-device AI for Mac, the data was never sent in the first place. For a deeper look at the privacy angle, see [private AI writing assistants that work offline](/guides/private-ai-writing-assistant-offline).

## On-device vs cloud AI

Cloud AI is powerful and convenient, but every request ships your text to someone else's servers to be processed. On-device AI keeps that text on your hardware. The trade-off is real: cloud models are often larger and a bit sharper, while local models run on whatever your Mac can spare. For most everyday writing and reading tasks the gap is small, and for sensitive work the privacy gain outweighs it. The smart approach is to choose per task rather than committing to one mode for everything.

## Why on-device matters for privacy

When the model runs locally, your text has no chance to be logged, breached, subpoenaed, or used to train a future model. That matters most for legal language, medical and financial notes, salary discussions, security details, and unreleased product information. A cloud provider may be entirely trustworthy, but on-device removes the need to decide — the confidential copy simply never exists outside your Mac. For privacy-conscious professionals, that certainty is the whole point.

## Apple Silicon makes it practical

A few years ago, running a capable model on a laptop was painful. Apple Silicon changed that: the unified memory and neural engine in modern Macs let small and mid-size local models respond quickly enough for real work. Tools like Ollama make it straightforward to download a [local LLM for writing on a Mac](/guides/local-llm-for-writing-mac) and point an app at it. You pick a model that fits your memory, and everyday tasks feel responsive rather than sluggish.

## What on-device AI is good for day to day

The clearest wins are the small, frequent tasks. Understand: select a confusing sentence and get it explained in plain words. Rewrite: polish your own draft so it reads natural and professional without changing your meaning. Reply: draft a response to a message in your own style. [Nugumi](/) is a macOS menu-bar app that can do all three with a fully local model, so the text you work on stays on your Mac while you move between Slack, Gmail, Notion, and any other app with selectable text.

## When you still want the cloud

On-device is the right default for sensitive material, but you don't have to give up cloud power entirely. The most flexible setup lets you keep a local model as your private baseline and switch to a ChatGPT or Claude subscription — or your own API key — for the occasional non-confidential draft that deserves maximum polish. Matching the engine to the text means you never overpay in privacy for a casual note, or in quality for an important one. For the offline side specifically, the [offline AI writing app guide](/guides/offline-ai-writing-app-no-internet) covers what works with no internet at all.

## Getting started on your Mac

The fastest way to understand on-device AI is to try it. Install Ollama, pull a small model, then [download](/download) Nugumi and point it at that local model. Select some text in any app, trigger the assistant, and watch it explain, rewrite, or reply — all without a network request. It is free during early beta with no account, so you can confirm on your own real, sensitive work that your text stays both useful and entirely on your machine.
