BLUF: To fix grammar and tone in any app on your Mac, use a system-wide menu-bar tool that acts on text you select anywhere — not a browser extension or a separate editor you have to paste into. You highlight a sentence in Slack, Gmail, Notion, or a PDF, trigger a fix, and paste the result back without leaving the window. The trick is choosing a tool that handles tone as well as grammar, adapts to the app you are in, and lets you decide how your text is processed. This guide walks through what to look for and how to decide quickly.

## Why "any app" is the real requirement

You write in dozens of places on a Mac, and most of them are not the browser: native Slack, KakaoTalk, Telegram, Discord, VS Code, Notes, Mail, and PDFs. A tool that only works in one editor or one browser tab forces a copy-paste-back loop that breaks your focus dozens of times a day. The honest test of any grammar and tone fixer for Mac is not how polished it looks in its own window, but whether it helps you in the window you are already typing in.

## Grammar is the easy half — tone is where it goes wrong

Spell-check and grammar fixes are largely solved, and most tools do them well. Tone is the harder, higher-stakes half: a grammatically perfect message can still read as blunt, pushy, or unsure, and that is where writing most often goes wrong for non-native speakers. To improve writing tone in every app on mac, pick a tool that rewrites a too-direct line into something that reads natural and professional — not one that only adds red underlines and leaves the rest to you. Sending a [clear, professional Slack message](/guides/professional-slack-messages-non-native-speaker) often comes down to tone, not spelling.

## How to fix grammar and tone without leaving the window

Prioritize a system-wide grammar and tone tool for Mac that operates on selected text rather than one tied to a browser extension or a single app. The flow should be simple: select any sentence, trigger one action, paste the result back. That removes the constant context-switching and means you can fix tone in any Mac app — a code review comment, a customer reply, a contract clause — the same way every time, instead of learning a different workflow for each app.

## Match the action to what you are doing

Fixing grammar and tone is not one task — it is a few related ones. Sometimes you want to polish your own rough draft; sometimes you need to draft a reply to a message someone sent you; sometimes you just need to understand what a dense paragraph actually means before you respond. A strong tool covers all three and adapts to context, so it reads casual in a group chat and professional in a client email. The same approach helps when you need to [politely decline a request in Slack](/guides/politely-decline-request-slack) without sounding cold.

## Keep your text private while you fix it

How your text is processed matters as much as the fix itself, especially at work. The strongest tools let you choose the engine: a ChatGPT or Claude subscription you already pay for, your own API key, or a fully local model so nothing you select ever leaves your Mac. Match the choice to the document — cloud is fine for everyday messages, but for contracts, client names, or internal plans, an on-device model that fixes grammar and tone offline is the safer default.
