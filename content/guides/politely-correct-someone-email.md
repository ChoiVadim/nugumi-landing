BLUF: To politely correct someone in an email, open with something neutral, state the correction as a fact rather than a judgment, give the correct information clearly, and offer help if it is useful. Keep the focus on the work, not the person, and stay short. A calm, specific note reads as helpful; words like "actually" and "you're wrong" read as superior, even when the facts are on your side.

## When to send a correction by email

Correct something in writing when the error affects a number, a decision, a deadline, or what other people will do next — and when a clear record helps everyone stay aligned. For a tiny typo with no real impact, it is often kinder to let it go or mention it in a quick chat. Email is the right channel when the correction needs to reach people who were not in the original conversation, or when the corrected information must be on record. If the mistake is sensitive or could embarrass someone, a quiet private message almost always lands better than a reply-all.

## The structure of a polite correction

A polite way to correct a mistake in an email follows a reliable shape. First, open with something neutral or appreciative so the reader does not feel attacked. Second, name the issue as a fact, not a fault: "the date in the invite looks like it should be the 12th, not the 21st." Third, give the correct information clearly so there is no guesswork. Fourth, offer a next step or help, then close briefly. This order keeps the tone collaborative and makes it easy for the other person to fix things without feeling blamed.

## Words and phrases that soften the message

When you point out an error, small wording choices decide whether it reads as helpful or rude. Lead with softeners: "I think there may be a small mix-up," "just flagging this," "I might be wrong, but," or "to double-check." Frame uncertain points as questions — "could you confirm the figure here?" — which invites a fix instead of demanding one. Avoid "actually," "you're wrong," "as I already said," and "FYI" used sharply; these signal superiority. The goal is to tell someone they made a mistake politely, so they feel informed, not corrected.

## A short example you can adapt

Subject: Quick check on the Q3 numbers

"Hi Sam, thanks for putting the report together so quickly. I think there may be a small mix-up in the revenue row — it shows 42k, but the source sheet has 24k. Could you take a look and confirm which is right? Happy to walk through it together if that's easier." Notice it opens warmly, states the issue as a possibility rather than a verdict, gives the specific numbers, and offers help. That is the whole job of how to politely correct someone in an email.

## Mistakes to avoid when correcting someone

Do not pile on. Listing every small error at once feels like an attack, even when each point is fair — flag what matters and let the rest go. Do not correct someone publicly when a private reply would do; an audience turns a helpful note into criticism. Avoid sarcasm and exclamation marks, which read as passive-aggressive in text. And do not over-soften to the point of confusion — being so vague that the reader misses the actual correction helps no one. ## After you send the correction

A good correction usually ends the matter, so resist the urge to keep re-explaining. Give the other person room to fix it, and thank them when they do — a short "thanks for sorting that so fast" keeps the relationship warm and makes the next correction easier. Handled well, correcting someone is not a confrontation — it is just two people getting the work right together.
