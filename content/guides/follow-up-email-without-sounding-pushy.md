BLUF: To write a follow-up email without sounding pushy, wait a reasonable interval, keep the message short, open with context instead of pressure, give the reader an easy way to respond, and close with one clear, low-stakes ask. Tone matters more than length — a calm, specific note reads as professional, while urgency and guilt read as demanding.

## When to send a follow-up email

Timing is the difference between a polite nudge and a pushy one. For most work threads, wait two to three business days after your original message before following up; for anything tied to a deadline, match the urgency to the real stakes, not your own anxiety. Sending a reminder the next morning almost always reads as impatient. If you have already followed up once with no reply, give it a few more days and ask whether email is still the right channel.

## Keep it short and lead with context

A pushy follow-up email is usually a long one that repeats everything and piles on pressure. Keep yours to three or four sentences. Open by referencing your previous message so the reader has context — "I wanted to follow up on the proposal I sent Tuesday" — rather than opening with "You still haven't replied." Leading with context signals that you are organized and considerate, not keeping score. A gentle reminder email works because it lowers the reader's effort, not because it raises the volume.

## Words and phrases that sound pushy

Certain phrases quietly raise the temperature. "As I already mentioned," "per my last email," "ASAP," and "I need this today" all read as accusatory or demanding, even when you do not mean them that way. Swap them for softer, specific alternatives: "just checking in," "when you have a moment," or "is there anything you need from me?" The goal is to follow up without being annoying — patient and helpful in tone, while still clear about what you are asking for.

## A simple follow-up email structure

Use a structure you can reuse every time: a one-line context reminder, a one-line reason it matters, and a single clear ask. For example: "Following up on the budget draft from last week. We need sign-off before the vendor call on Friday. Could you let me know if it looks good, or what you'd change?" That is polite, specific, and easy to answer in one reply. Avoid stacking multiple questions — one ask tends to get answered, five tend to get ignored.

## Give the reader an easy way out

People often go quiet because they are busy or unsure, not because they are ignoring you. Make it easy to say "not yet" without friction: "No rush if now isn't a good time" or "Happy to wait until next week if that's easier." This removes the guilt-trip feeling and, in practice, often gets faster replies. ## What to do after a second no-response

If two well-spaced, polite follow-ups go unanswered, stop sending the same email. Change the channel — a short Slack message or a quick call — or loop in someone who can unblock the decision. You can also send a calm "closing the loop" note: "I'll assume this isn't a priority right now and check back next month — let me know if that changes." That keeps the door open without nagging.
