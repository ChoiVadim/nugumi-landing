BLUF: A privacy-focused Grammarly alternative that doesn't store your data is one that either runs entirely on your own machine or sends your text to a provider that keeps nothing after the request. Grammarly and most browser-based checkers route everything you type through their servers, which is a problem for confidential, legal, medical, or client work. The most private option is a local model that never sends your words off-device at all; the next best is a cloud model under a no-retention agreement you control. For professionals handling sensitive text, choose a tool by where your data goes, not by feature count.

## Why data storage is the real privacy question

Most writing tools quietly upload what you type. Grammarly processes your text on its servers, and many AI extensions log prompts to improve their own models. For anyone drafting contracts, patient notes, unreleased product specs, or internal strategy, that upload is the risk—not the grammar quality. A privacy-focused Grammarly alternative that doesn't store your data removes the question entirely: if the text never leaves your computer, there is nothing to leak, subpoena, or breach.

## What "doesn't store your data" actually means

The phrase covers three very different things. True on-device processing means your text never travels over the network. Zero-retention cloud means a provider receives the text but deletes it immediately and never trains on it. And "we don't sell your data" is the weakest promise—it often still means indefinite storage. When you evaluate a secure Grammarly alternative, read the policy for the word retention, not just privacy, and check whether training on your inputs is off by default rather than something you have to opt out of.

## How to vet a private Grammarly alternative

Ask five concrete questions. Does it offer a local-only mode? If it uses the cloud, what is the retention window and is it zero by default? Can you bring your own API key so the data goes straight to a provider you already trust? Is it open source, so the data flow is auditable? And does it work without an account, so there is no profile tying your writing to your identity? A tool that answers well on all five is a genuinely private Grammarly alternative; one that dodges the retention question is not.

## Local, on-device options (strongest privacy)

The only way to guarantee nothing is stored is to keep the text on your machine. Local models via Ollama now run capable open-weight LLMs on a modern Mac, so you can fix grammar, polish tone, and draft replies with nothing leaving the device. [Nugumi](/) supports this directly: point it at local Ollama and everything you select stays on your own computer—no account, no upload, nothing to retain. It is open source, which means the data path is something you can verify rather than take on faith.

## Cloud options with no-retention policies

On-device is not always practical—older hardware or very long documents may need a hosted model. Here the goal shifts to control. Using your own OpenAI or Anthropic API key sends text directly to a provider under terms that typically include zero retention and no training on your inputs, instead of through a third-party middleman that may log it. This is a reasonable middle ground for a secure Grammarly alternative when local models fall short. ## Where a system-wide, private tool fits

Privacy and convenience usually trade off, but they don't have to. A menu-bar assistant that acts on selected text in any app can be both. With a local backend, you can select a sensitive paragraph in Mail, Notion, or a PDF and have it explained, rewritten, or replied to in place, without copying it into a browser tool that logs everything. Nugumi works this way across Slack, Gmail, Safari, and code editors, and it lets each user pick local, subscription, or own-key—so the privacy posture is your decision, not the vendor's. ## How to choose

Pick by your strictest constraint. If the text is truly confidential, use a local model so it never leaves your machine. If you need a hosted model's quality, bring your own key under a zero-retention policy. Avoid any tool that stores prompts indefinitely or trains on them by default. For most privacy-conscious professionals, a free, open-source, system-wide tool with a local mode is the cleanest fit—[download](/download) one, run it fully offline, and confirm with your own network monitor that nothing leaves the device before you trust it with real work.
