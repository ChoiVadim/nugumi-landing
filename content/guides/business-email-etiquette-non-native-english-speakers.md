BLUF: Business email etiquette for non-native English speakers comes down to five habits: a clear subject line, a greeting that fits your relationship with the reader, a direct but warm tone, short one-idea paragraphs, and a polite close that states the next step. You do not need perfect, native-level English to write emails people respect and reply to quickly. You need to be clear, respectful, and easy to act on. This guide covers the conventions that matter most at work and the small fixes that remove the friction readers notice.

## Start with a clear subject line and the right greeting

Your subject line should tell the reader what the email is about and what you want, in a few words: "Invoice #204 — confirmation needed by Friday." A vague subject like "Quick question" gets opened late and answered later. Open with a greeting that fits the context — "Hi [Name]," works for most colleagues, while "Dear [Name]," suits formal or first-contact messages. Skip "To whom it may concern" unless you genuinely do not know who will read it.

## Match the level of formality to your reader

Formality is the part of business email etiquette that trips up many non-native English speakers, because the rules differ from their first language. A message to a senior client is more formal than a quick note to a teammate you message every day. When you are unsure, start slightly more formal and relax as the other person sets the tone. Mirroring how they write — their greeting, their sign-off, whether they use first names — is a reliable shortcut to getting it right.

## Be direct without sounding rude

English business writing values directness, but direct does not mean blunt. Soften requests with a short frame: "Could you send the file by Thursday?" reads far better than "Send the file by Thursday." A simple "thank you" or "I appreciate your help" carries real weight. ## Keep sentences short and one idea per paragraph

Long, dense sentences are the most common reason a clear message turns confusing. Aim for one idea per sentence and one topic per paragraph, and use a bulleted list when you have three or more points. Short paragraphs are easier to read on a phone, where most work email is now opened. Clean structure also makes your English look more confident, even when the words you use are simple.

## Close politely and make the next step obvious

End by telling the reader exactly what you need and by when: "Please confirm by Wednesday so we can book the venue." Then sign off in a way that matches the formality — "Best regards" for formal, "Thanks" or "Best" for everyday. A clear close prevents the back-and-forth that wastes everyone's time and signals respect for the reader's attention. If the email asks for nothing, say that too, so no one wonders whether a reply is expected.

## Proofread for tone, not just grammar

A grammatically correct email can still feel cold, pushy, or unclear. Before you send, read it once asking, "How will this feel to the person receiving it?" ## Quick fixes for common mistakes

A few patterns appear again and again. Replace "Please do the needful" with a specific request, and drop "Kindly" from the start of every sentence — once is plenty. Avoid all-caps and rows of exclamation marks, which read as shouting rather than enthusiasm. Give the reader a line of context before the ask, so the request does not arrive cold.
